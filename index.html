Entering the forest

Between the dry leaves and the serene morning, there are small engineers, masters of disguise, and nutrient recyclers. They are important to keep the planet running, as well as the ecosystem and biogeochemical exchanges. Fungi, insects and amphibians they are the three pillars that support the forest. Cataloging these beings is our way of understanding this small world.

Fungi

They are not plants or animals! Fungi are fundamental to the functioning of ecosystems, being primary decomposers of organic matter and responsible for the recycling of nutrients. They create invisible networks under the earth, taking their nutrients from the tree with its mycelium.

Insects

Insects form the largest class of the Animal Kingdom, there are more than 800,000 known species. Insects live scattered all over the world. From the polar regions to tropical zones, passing through rivers, seas and oceans.

Although the appearance of insects is very varied, the body of all of them is divided into head, chest and abdomen. On the head are a pair of antennae and 3 pairs of jaws. All insects have 3 pairs of legs. Not all insects have wings. Most of these animals reach maturity through metamorphosis.

Amphibians

They are animals of great evolutionary and ecological success, occurring on almost every continent of the world (with the exception of Antarctica). They occupy various types of humid environments, including forests, fields, and a wide variety of water bodies.

They are ectothermal animals, that is, they use external sources of heat to regulate their body temperature and thus carry out their vital functions. Because they are highly sensitive to environmental changes, they are considered bioindicators; in other words, their presence in a place can indicate whether the environment is in balance or ecological imbalance.

Tiny but Important


The coexistence of fungi, insects, and amphibians in moist soil is what keeps the forest active. This wiki brings together these three groups because they do not survive in isolation, where the integrity of one depends directly on the presence of the other in the same ecosystem. The purpose of this space is to document these tiny beings. Cataloging these interactions together is our starting point to understand how small variations at this level affect the balance of the entire surrounding environment.
