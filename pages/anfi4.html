Family: Bufonidae
Order: Anura (frogs and toads)
Genus: Rhinella

Name: Cururu toad (or Schneider's toad)

Scientific name: Rhinella diptycha

Habitat

Open formations, in savannas, fields, pastures, riparian forests, edges of gallery forests, meadows, and swamps.

Toxicity

Like most buphonids, this species is able to secrete venom, with the aim of fending off predators such as snakes. It has an extremely resistant venom, with cardiotoxic characteristics due to its similarity to cardioglycosides. Can cause from nausea and vomiting to paralysis and death. Unlike many myths about the species, it is unable to purposefully squirt poison, requiring its glands to be pressed, with which it is harmless to humans, since it would be necessary that this was done and that the toxin was ingested or came into contact with the mucous membranes so that it could cause any harm.

Diet

Insects, arthropods and small vertebrates (mice and snakes).

Description

The cururu toad (scientific name: Rhinella diptycha, old nomenclature: Rhinella schneideri), also known as the booby frog, is a species of anurus in the family Bufonidae. It is native to Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, Paraguay and Uruguay. It is found in the biomes of the Chaco, Atlantic Forest, Pantanal, Caatinga and Cerrado, being seen mainly in open areas and peridomiciliary. Males average 13 centimeters and females 15, being the largest species of anurus in South America. Its color varies from light brown to dark, with brown spots. It has the paratoid and paracnenemic glands quite developed, which makes it different from other species. It does not have evident sexual dimorphism. When dimpled, it has a black and oval body, with a short, transparent tail. The egg has an average of 1.8 millimeters.

It has a basically carnivorous diet, feeding on invertebrates such as insects and spiders, and vertebrates such as birds and rodents. The species also feeds on plant matter, but it is not known whether the intake is purposive. Breeds between July and October, with the male vocalizing to attract females. It is one of the few species that do not vocalize only in the reproductive period. Its amplexo is axillary and can last up to 40 hours for oviposition. The eggs are deposited in a gelatinous cord, containing more than 5,000 eggs, which are attached to aquatic plants. When eggs hatch, the tadpoles form groups of tens or hundreds of individuals.
