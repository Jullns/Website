Climate zones: Tropical climate
Family: Nymphalidae
Suborder: Heliconiinae

Name: Julia/ Flambeau

Scientific name: Dryas iulia

Habits

Dryas iulia butterflies are usually seen flying on trails of humid and deciduous forests, but are commonly found in open and anthropic areas such as forest clearings, pastures, along riverbanks and in flower gardens. They feed, often collectively, on mineralized substances taken from stones, soil, fruits, animals such as alligators or crocodiles, and substances taken from flowers like the Lantana camara.

Appearance

Individuals of this species have long and narrow wings, with a little more than 80 millimetres in wingspan, and are orange or dark yellow, seen dorsally (at the top), with a brownish stripe, more or less pronounced, crossing the upper part of the anterior wings and another much smaller, forming a sharp triangle, just above. They have mild sexual dimorphism, with females smaller and darker than males. Seen ventrally (below), they have a dry leaf pattern, with or without spots.

Description

Individuals of this species have long and narrow wings, with a little more than 80 millimetres in wingspan, and are orange or dark yellow, seen dorsally (at the top), with a brownish stripe, more or less pronounced, crossing the upper part of the anterior wings and another much smaller, forming a sharp triangle, just above. They have mild sexual dimorphism, with females smaller and darker than males. Seen ventrally (below), they have a dry leaf pattern, with or without spots.

Dryas iulia eggs are placed in plants of the genus Passiflora (Maracujá/passion fruit) and are yellow to orange-opaque. Their larvae or caterpillars pass through five stages. Dryas iulia presents one of the most complex and variable coloring patterns among first-instar larvae in Heliconiinae. The cephalic capsule is brownish-yellow, remainder of the body slightly lighter. Intestinal content, visible by transparency after feeding, generating a greenish-brown band. White spots and dark brown stripes distributed through the chest and abdomen. Dark brown-brown cephalic capsule bristles at the base and translucent apex. Black chest and abdomen bristles, with dark brown base and hyaline terminal portion. In their last larval stage, they are predominantly white to cream colored, with black stripes, reddish spots on the side, and with thorny projections on the surface. According to Otero, they are aggressive and cannibalistic. The chrysalis is not uniform in its coloration, consisting of various shades of brown and gray. The egg of Dryas iulia alcionea is yellow to orange-opaque, with brown spots near hatching.
