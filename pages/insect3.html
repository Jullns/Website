Family: Lucanidae
Order: Coleoptera
Suborder:

Name: Stag beetle

Scientific name: Lucanos cervus

Habitat

It occurs in forests and deciduous woods with large trees, especially oaks and, among these, shows preference for English oak (Quercus robur). It can also be found in parks and gardens.

Distribution

It is distributed throughout Europe and the Middle East. In mainland Portugal, its presence is recorded from the north of the territory to the Serra de Sintra.

Reproduction

Males and females are quite distinct in this adult stage. While the males have large jaws (to fight rival males), brown-reddish in color, females have black and small jaws. The males exhibit themselves on top of the trees, becoming visible to the females and this elevated position allows them to bring down rival males by throwing them to the ground. After mating, the males die and the females seek "safe" places to lay eggs that ensure the survival of the new generation. After the posture, they die too.

Description

It is the largest beetle in Europe; the male measures 2.7 to 5.3 cm (excluding jaws), and the female, 2.6 to 4.1 cm long. Reddish-brown to dark brown tegument. Antennae of 6 articles, of which the first 2 are long and the last 4 are in the form of overlapping lamellae, arranged in clava. Sexual dimorphism: the male displays large jaws, much larger than those of the female, which serves them in fights between rivals. Adults feed on sugared exudates from trees or fruits. It is a polyphagous and saproxylic species: the larvae are melolontiform and feed on decaying wood; they can reach 10 cm. Adults emerge between March and May and live until October.

The female digs a deep gallery, at the bottom of which she lays eggs together with a secretion containing yeasts. Each pose has about 20 eggs and each female can do several postures. These yeasts will allow the larvae to digest wood. The larval period lasts 3 to 4 years. In the 3rd stadium, the larva moves to the ground where it builds a cocoon to pupate. The adult is fully formed in autumn, but only hatches in spring or early summer. The period of dispersal in adult form occurs between May and September. Individuals are active, especially in the twilight period.
