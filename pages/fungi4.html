Family: Lyophyllaceae
Order: Agaricales
Genus: Lyophyllum

Name: Shimeji

Scientific name: Lyophyllum shimeji

Ecological importance

Like the other forest fungi, shimeji plays a fundamental role in nature. It is a decomposing organism (saprophyte). This means that it feeds on dead organic matter (such as old wood and leaves), breaking down complex molecules and returning essential nutrients to the soil, helping to keep the forest alive and fertile.

Cooking and Nutrition

In the kitchen, shimeji is highly valued for its umami flavor (the fifth basic taste of the human palate, which refers to something flavorful and full-bodied). It absorbs seasonings such as soy sauce (shoyu), butter, garlic and chives very well, being prepared quickly in the pan on high heat.
In addition to being delicious, it is an excellent food for health:
Low in calories and fats, ideal for a balanced diet; Rich in plant-based proteins and dietary fibers; Contains B vitamins and important minerals such as potassium and phosphorus.

Description

Shimeji mushrooms are a species of edible fungus, Hypsizygus tessellatus, which are native to East Asia but are cultivated in North America, Europe, and Australia, where they are known as beech mushrooms. They have small round caps and long, slender stems that grow from an interconnected base. They're often found growing in dead or decaying beech trees, which is where they get their name, as well as in cottonwood and elm trees. There are a number of varieties of shimeji mushrooms, though the most common are the white shimeji, which is also known as white beech mushroom, white clamshell mushroom, or Bunapi-shimeji; and the brown shimeji, also known as brown beech, brown clamshell, or Buna-shimeji mushrooms. The colors are analogous to white button mushrooms and brown cremini mushrooms.

Shimeji mushrooms have a crunchy texture, and a savory, nutty, umami flavor, due to the high concentration of naturally occurring amino acids known as glutamates they contain,1 much like foods, such as parmesan cheese and anchovies. While shimeji mushrooms in the wild grow from fallen hardwood trees, commercially cultivated ones are grown in a bed of grain, sawdust, and other organic materials.

Although there are different varieties, the two easiest to find in everyday life are:
Shimeji Black (or Gray): It has a slightly more robust taste and an extremely soft texture when cooked. It is the most traditional version. White Shimeji: A variation obtained through genetic selection and light control during cultivation. It has a slightly milder and more delicate taste than black.
