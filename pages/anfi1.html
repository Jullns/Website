Family: Ambystomatidae
Order: Caudata (the salamander group)

Name: Axolotl

Scientific name: Ambystoma mexicanum

Habitat

Occurs in lakes, where it hides in aquatic vegetation and mud.

Insects, molluscs, worms, fish and zooplankton.

The axolot (Ambystoma mexicanum) is an amphibian found only in freshwater lakes of Mexico, and only Lake Xochimilco remains as a natural habitat.
The wild individuals are green and brown, with dark spots on the body. However, the axolotes of commercial catifiers are often leucistic (white) or other vibrant polymorphisms. They have external gills that come out of the neck, a common feature of all salamander larvae but which remains in the axolote due to the more aquatic lifestyle. His face is wide, with small eyes and a big mouth.
These animals range from 15 to 45 cm in length, and weigh on average around 300 g.

Due mainly to habitat loss, the axolot is critically endangered. Lake Chalco, one of its native bodies of water, was fully drained, along with other large Mexican lakes, to prevent flooding. Today, they occur naturally only in Lake Xochimilco, near Mexico City, which has been fragmented into channel systems and suffers from high levels of pollution due to urbanization and tourism. In these places, invasive fish species compete directly with the axolotes for the few remaining resources. Although axolot is very common in the pet trade, it is believed that this population is entirely bred in captivity, so capture for the wildlife trade no longer puts pressure on wild axolots.
