Family: Meloídeos
Order: Coleoptera
Suborder: Polyphaga

Name: Spanish Fly

Scientific name: Lytta vesicatoria

Location

It is a species present in the Portuguese and Spanish territories. In Portugal, they tend to be present in the northern region.

Diet

The adults sometimes feed on flowers and leaves of plants from several families, such as Amaranthaceae , Asteraceae , Fabaceae and Solanaceae . Many beetles from this family are considered agricultural pests.

Behavior

It is a quiet animal when adult, it is not recommended to handle it, although it is possible, as long as it does not get agitated/frightened and does not secrete poison.
If they feel threatened, in addition to exhaling a very strong smell so that predators do not approach them, they release a poison that burns the skin forming bubbles. This one is among the most venomous beetles there are.

Toxin

The elixirs of this species are particularly rich in cantharidin, a substance that is used to prepare cantonide, a toxic product with diuretic and vesicant properties present in soft parts and circulating liquid, which at high doses can be lethal.
Historically, cantharidin had been reputed as an aphrodisiac, probably induced by the observation of pelvic congestion and priapism, which arise in the aftermath of intoxication with this substance. At present, such properties are not recognized.
The use of cantharidin by these species works as a defense and sexual attraction in the form of pheromone. Cantharidin is produced and synthesized by the male of the species, however in most species females do not produce cantharidin.

Descriptions of the use of cantharidin in traditional medicine date back to antiquity, philosophers such as Hippocrates, Celsus and Pliny mention this drug, which was thought to be a good remedy for the cure of gout, anthrax, rheumatism and other medical disorders. It is known that the "paste" made from the macerated body of beetles is also an irritant of the urinary tract, but in antiquity it was used as a powerful aphrodisiac, especially used by gentlemen in the middle ages.
This toxin is absorbed by the skin being highly toxic to mammals. The amount of cantharidin in adult insects varies from 1% to 5.4%, and it is believed that cantharidin confers protection against predators; females store cantharidin from males and disperse on their eggs.
